
## Evaluation, Reliability, and Deployment

### Evaluation

Evaluation of biomedical foundation models should be task-centered rather than model-centered. The central question is not whether a model scores highly on a generic leaderboard, but whether it improves performance on the biomedical task that actually matters, such as diagnosis, prognosis, survival prediction, retrieval, report generation, or biomarker discovery [@agrawal2025evaluation; @wornow2023shaky]. This requires clearly specifying the intended use case, the target population, the relevant comparator, and the consequences of model errors. Recent clinical benchmarking work makes this point clearly: models that perform well on general leaderboards do not necessarily perform best on clinical decision-making tasks, and there is still no single widely accepted benchmark for clinical utility [@sandmann2025deepseek].

Evaluation must also go beyond discrimination alone. Metrics such as AUROC, AUPRC, F1, concordance index, and segmentation overlap are informative, but they are not sufficient for high-stakes biomedical use. Methodological reviews in medical imaging emphasize that proper assessment must also consider calibration, uncertainty, and other task-specific criteria, because a model can appear accurate on average while still producing poorly calibrated or clinically misleading outputs [@kocak2025evaluationmetrics]. The choice of metric should therefore follow the downstream use case: prediction tasks require calibration and risk stratification, retrieval and generation tasks require expert judgment of quality and utility, and discovery-oriented applications require reproducibility and biological plausibility in addition to raw predictive performance.

External validation is equally important. Biomedical data are heterogeneous across hospitals, populations, devices, and time, so single-dataset testing rarely provides persuasive evidence of real-world value. Recent foundation-model studies are increasingly adopting broader evaluation strategies in response. For example, PanDerm was evaluated on 28 benchmarks spanning screening, diagnosis, segmentation, longitudinal monitoring, and prognosis using multimodal dermatology data collected from 11 institutions [@yan2025panderm]. In computational pathology, a recent benchmark evaluated 19 histopathology foundation models on 13 patient cohorts comprising 6,818 patients and 9,528 slides, explicitly emphasizing external and clinically relevant tasks [@neidlinger2025benchmarking]. These studies point toward a more appropriate standard for biomedical foundation model evaluation: multi-cohort, multi-task, and out-of-distribution assessment rather than isolated internal validation.

Benchmark design itself also matters. If test sets are contaminated by publicly available pretraining data, reported performance may exaggerate true progress. Recent pathology benchmarking work addressed this concern by withholding test data and exposing only an automated evaluation pipeline, explicitly citing contamination risk as a reason not to release the test cohorts [@campanella2025clinicalbenchmark]. This issue is especially acute for foundation models, whose large-scale pretraining makes overlap difficult to rule out. Fair evaluation should therefore compare biomedical foundation models not only against older baselines, but also against strong task-specific models, smaller domain models, and adapted general foundation models under contamination-aware protocols [@campanella2025clinicalbenchmark; @agrawal2025evaluation]. In many settings, the most relevant comparator is not the largest available model, but the strongest clinically realistic alternative.

Finally, retrospective benchmarking should not be treated as the endpoint of evaluation. For high-stakes biomedical applications, evidence becomes much stronger when evaluation extends toward expert assessment, workflow-aware analysis, and prospective study. A prospective study of open-source melanoma AI showed that algorithmic performance could be examined in clinical practice and that the system had potential to improve dermatologist decision-making, while still requiring larger randomized studies before routine adoption [@marchetti2023proveai]. More broadly, recent work on clinical implementation argues that the value of an AI system is established only when predictive performance is connected to usability, workflow fit, and patient-relevant outcomes [@rosen2025clinicalimplementation]. A strong evaluation framework for biomedical foundation models should therefore progress from retrospective benchmarks to external validation, expert review, and, where feasible, prospective assessment in the intended environment.

### Reliability

Reliability is a stricter requirement than good benchmark performance. In the biomedical setting, reliability concerns whether model outputs remain trustworthy when deployment conditions depart from the development setting and whether failures are sufficiently visible, bounded, and manageable for high-stakes use. This matters especially for foundation models, which are often promoted as flexible systems that can transfer across tasks and modalities, even though that same flexibility complicates validation and oversight [@moor2023foundation; @agrawal2025evaluation]. Reliability should therefore be understood as a combination of robustness under shift, appropriate uncertainty, and protection against clinically consequential failure modes rather than as a simple extension of retrospective accuracy.

A defining reliability problem in biomedicine is distribution shift. Clinical data vary across hospitals, scanners, laboratories, note-writing styles, patient populations, and calendar time, so strong internal performance may not survive external deployment. Recent evidence suggests that this is not a marginal issue. Ong Ly et al. reported across 13 medical datasets that apparent model performance was overestimated by up to 20% on average because models exploited hidden data-acquisition biases rather than disease-relevant signal [@ong2024shortcut]. Related work in medical imaging showed that fairness assessed in the original distribution can break down under external generalization, indicating that subgroup reliability cannot be assumed even when a model appears well behaved during development [@yang2024limits]. These findings imply that reliability evaluation must explicitly examine external generalization, subgroup stability, and resistance to shortcut learning rather than assuming that scale or domain pretraining automatically resolves them.

Reliability also requires models to indicate when they are likely to fail. In clinical settings, an incorrect answer delivered with high confidence may be more dangerous than no answer at all. Banerji et al. argue that clinical AI tools should communicate predictive uncertainty at the level of the individual patient, not only through population-level summary statistics [@banerji2023uncertainty]. Recent work on responsible deployment similarly emphasizes selective prediction or selective deployment, in which models abstain, defer, or are restricted in settings where generalization is uncertain, particularly for underrepresented groups or patient-facing use cases [@goetz2024generalization]. For biomedical foundation models, reliability should therefore include uncertainty estimation, abstention behavior, and escalation pathways to clinicians or downstream testing. A model that is accurate on average but unable to express uncertainty or defer appropriately is not yet reliable enough for routine biomedical use.

These issues become even more acute for generative biomedical foundation models. In tasks such as note summarization, report drafting, or clinical question answering, the central reliability issue is not whether the output sounds plausible, but whether it is faithful, complete, and clinically safe. Tam et al. reviewed 142 studies on human evaluation of healthcare LLMs and found substantial gaps in the reliability, generalizability, and applicability of current human-evaluation practice, arguing for more standardized expert-centered assessment [@tam2024human]. More directly, Asgari et al. developed a framework for evaluating hallucinations and omissions in medical text summarization and found that omissions were more common than hallucinations, whereas hallucinations were more likely to be major errors with potential downstream clinical harm [@asgari2025clinicalsafety]. In this setting, reliability cannot be established through automated metrics alone; it requires clinician review, structured failure-mode analysis, and explicit assessment of whether model errors could alter care in unsafe ways.

Taken together, recent work suggests that the core reliability question is not whether biomedical foundation models are impressive under idealized conditions, but whether they remain trustworthy once reality becomes messy. If robustness under shift is weak, uncertainty is poorly expressed, or generative errors are difficult to detect before they influence care, then the case for biomedical foundation models weakens considerably. Their value depends not only on broader capability, but on whether that capability can be made dependable in the settings where biomedical decisions are actually made [@moor2023foundation; @agrawal2025evaluation].

### Deployment

Deployment is where the central question of this review becomes most concrete. Even when a biomedical foundation model performs well in retrospective studies and appears reasonably reliable, it is not necessarily useful unless it can be integrated into real biomedical systems and workflows. Recent implementation work emphasizes that adoption depends on more than technical validity: it also requires workflow fit, comparison against existing practice, and attention to implementation outcomes rather than model accuracy alone [@vandesande2024multifaceted; @you2025clinicaltrials; @wells2025fairai]. Deployment should therefore be treated as a sociotechnical problem rather than as the final technical stage of model development.

A first requirement is that biomedical foundation models must support human work rather than merely produce outputs. In practice, successful deployment depends on whether the model can be embedded into existing decision pathways, whether users understand how to act on its outputs, and whether it reduces rather than increases cognitive or administrative burden. A recent Nature Medicine study on AI-supported decision support for colorectal cancer surgery used a stepwise design that moved from registry-based development to external validation and then to prospective clinical implementation with risk-tailored intervention bundles, explicitly linking model predictions to a treatment workflow [@rosen2025clinicalimplementation]. Related evidence suggests that clinician-led deployment studies are more likely to show meaningful impact than technologist-led studies, underscoring that organizational design and domain leadership shape whether AI tools become clinically useful [@li2025leadership]. More broadly, recent foundation-model work has begun to evaluate systems in expert workflows rather than in isolation, as in medical literature mining where human–AI collaboration was assessed through time savings and task completion rather than benchmark scores alone [@wang2025leads].

A second requirement is operational feasibility. Foundation models may be attractive in principle yet difficult to deploy in practice because of infrastructure demands, latency constraints, privacy requirements, and the burden of local governance. Large multimodal systems may require substantial compute, specialized hosting, or complex interfaces with EHR, LIS, PACS, or research data pipelines. In some settings, using external APIs may be incompatible with institutional privacy rules or data-governance policies, while local deployment may be costly and difficult to maintain. Model updates can create further challenges, because behavior may shift after re-training or vendor revision, requiring renewed validation and local review. For this reason, deployment decisions should account not only for predictive value but also for whether a model can be hosted, integrated, audited, and revalidated within the constraints of the target institution.

A third requirement is staged rollout. Because biomedical use is high stakes, recent frameworks increasingly recommend silent trials, controlled introduction, and prospective validation before routine deployment. You et al. propose a clinical-trials-inspired framework with four phases—safety, efficacy, effectiveness, and monitoring—and explicitly describe silent-mode testing as an early implementation stage in which predictions are observed without affecting care [@you2025clinicaltrials]. Rosenthal et al. similarly argue that adaptive medical AI systems, especially those involving large language models, require “dynamic deployment” with continuous real-time monitoring and clinical validation rather than a one-time linear approval framework [@rosenthal2025dynamicdeployment]. Real-world examples are beginning to emerge: in pathology, Campanella et al. reported deployment of an AI-assisted workflow in a four-month silent trial from May to August 2024 and evaluated its noninferiority under real clinical operating conditions [@campanella2025realworlddeployment]. Together, these studies suggest that deployment evidence for biomedical foundation models should be built progressively, from retrospective validation to silent testing to supervised real-world use.

Deployment also does not end once a model goes live. Post-deployment monitoring, governance, and maintenance are essential because biomedical data, workflows, and institutional practices drift over time. A recent review of monitoring methods for clinical AI found that evidence and guidance remain relatively sparse despite broad agreement that ongoing surveillance is necessary [@andersen2024monitoring]. Recent governance frameworks similarly emphasize that healthcare organizations need practical structures for addressing transparency, accountability, equity, privacy, safety, and robustness after deployment, not only before it [@saenz2024responsibleuse; @wells2025fairai]. This point is especially important for biomedical foundation models, whose broad capabilities may make failure modes harder to anticipate and whose updates may alter behavior in ways that are difficult to validate locally. The deployment case for biomedical foundation models is therefore strongest only when they can be integrated into workflows, introduced through staged implementation, and monitored throughout their operational lifecycle.
